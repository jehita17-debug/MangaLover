Nothing is wrong here, which is the whole problem. Meiko has a job she doesn't believe in, a boyfriend she half-believes in, and a life that fits her the way a borrowed coat fits — close enough that no one would notice it isn't hers. Asano draws this not as tragedy but as weather, the low grey ceiling that settles over the years just after you stop being a student and before you become whatever you're going to be.

Read this on the night when the question "is this it?" arrives and refuses to leave. It is not a manga about quitting your job to chase a dream; it is far more honest than that. It knows that the dream is also a kind of trap, that courage and self-deception look identical from the inside, and that the bravest thing in the room might be the person who simply keeps going.

Skip it if you need the story to tell you whether they were right. Asano won't. But if you have ever stood at the exact center of your own life and felt it as a kind of vertigo — the sense that all the doors are still technically open and that this, somehow, is the most frightening part — this one will sit with you for a long time afterward.

<!--SPOILER-->
The mid-book death is the hinge the entire work turns on, and Asano's refusal to dramatize it is what makes it unbearable. There is no lesson, no redemptive arc — just an absence the survivors have to build the rest of their lives around. When Meiko finally finishes the song, it doesn't resolve her grief so much as carry it forward in a form she can hold. That's the quiet thesis: you don't get over the things that mark you, you learn to play them.
