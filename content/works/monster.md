A brilliant surgeon makes a quiet moral choice — to save a child's life ahead of a politician's — and spends the rest of the manga discovering what that child becomes. Urasawa builds from this a sprawling, controlled, almost unbearably tense chase across Europe, but the engine underneath it is a single question that never lets up: is one human life inherently worth more than another, and what do you owe the world if your kindness sets something terrible loose?

Read this on the night you want a thriller that respects your intelligence completely — that trusts you to hold dozens of characters, to sit with ambiguity, to feel the weight of a decision across hundreds of chapters. The plotting is immaculate, but it's the moral seriousness that lingers: Urasawa never lets you off the hook, never lets evil be simple or comforting.

Skip it only if you want something short; this is a long, patient commitment. But if you can give it the room, Monster is one of the few thrillers that earns the word "novelistic" — a study of guilt, redemption, and the unsettling idea that monstrousness might be made rather than born, told with a craftsman's total command.

<!--SPOILER-->
Urasawa's masterstroke is keeping Johan mostly off-page, a vacuum the other characters fill with their own fears — so that when the question of his origin finally resolves, the answer indicts nurture and circumstance as much as any innate evil. Tenma's long refusal to become a killer, even to stop a killer, is the moral spine: the manga argues that the line between saving and destroying is held by people who refuse to cross it, no matter how reasonable crossing would be. The ambiguous final image keeps the wound open on purpose.
