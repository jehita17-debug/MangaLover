A shy, bullied kid gets pulled off the pavement by a passing boxer and asks a question he can't shake: what does it mean to be strong? The answer takes hundreds of chapters, and that length is the point. Few manga make you feel the cost of a skill the way this one does — the road work, the bag work, the small mechanical breakthrough that decides a fight months later.

Read this when you want sincerity without apology. Ippo is almost embarrassingly earnest, and the series rewards that earnestness with fights that are drawn like weather systems, all build and release. The supporting cast of rival boxers each get the dignity of a reason to fight, so the wins cost something and the losses teach something.

It is very long, and the comedy can swing broad between bouts. If you want a tight, finite story, this is the opposite of that. But if you're in the mood to sink into a world and watch a frightened boy turn himself into a fighter one honest round at a time, Hajime no Ippo is the warmest hard-hitting manga on the shelf.
