This is the hardest Asano to recommend and one of the most honest things he has drawn. Koume and Isobe are two bored, hurting teenagers in a coastal town who start a physical relationship as a way of not feeling alone — and the manga watches, without flinching and without judging, as the thing they reach for to feel less empty quietly makes them emptier. It is frank about sex in a way that is never titillating; the explicitness is the point, because Asano is showing you how mechanical closeness becomes when it is standing in for everything it can't replace.

Read this on the night you find yourself thinking, with more tenderness than you expected, about someone you used a little and who used you back. It is about the particular cruelty teenagers are capable of toward each other and themselves, and about how a small coastal nothing of a town can become the whole sky when you are sixteen and have nowhere to put your feelings.

Skip it if explicit content is a hard no for you — this one earns its rating and uses it deliberately, but it is genuinely adult. If you can sit with that, what you'll find underneath is one of the most clear-eyed accounts of adolescent loneliness in the medium: not a romance, not a cautionary tale, just two people failing to save each other in real time.

<!--SPOILER-->
The genius and the gut-punch is that nothing is resolved by the relationship — Isobe's grief over his brother doesn't heal, Koume's loneliness doesn't lift, and the ending offers no redemption arc, only the dull continuation of two lives that briefly collided. Asano denies you the catharsis of either tragedy or growth. What lingers is the sense that they were, for a while, the only person who saw the other at all, and that this was both real and not nearly enough.
