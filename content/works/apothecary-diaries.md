A pharmacist's daughter, sold into service at an imperial rear palace, can't help solving the poisonings and illnesses around her — even though every clever deduction risks exposing her and dragging her deeper into court politics she'd rather avoid. Maomao is the draw: blunt, incurious about status, lit up only by toxins and medicine, and far too smart to stay invisible for long.

Read this when you want the satisfaction of watching someone be quietly the smartest person in the room. The cases are real puzzles rooted in herbalism and human nature, the period setting is richly drawn, and the will-they tension with the enigmatic official who keeps recruiting her is handled with a long, dry patience that pays off.

If you want fast action or overt fantasy, this is neither — it's a character-and-deduction story that takes its time. But if you're in the mood for a heroine who treats palace glamour as an annoyance and a mysterious poison as a treat, The Apothecary Diaries is one of the most addictively re-readable series running, smart enough to make competence itself the romance.
