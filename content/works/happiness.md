A bullied, invisible boy is attacked one night and wakes up changed — and Oshimi, true to form, is far less interested in fangs and blood than in what it feels like to become permanently separate from everyone you know. The horror here is social before it is supernatural: the dawning understanding that you can no longer pretend to be like the others, that the gap you always felt has become literal and irreversible.

Read this on the night you remember adolescence as a kind of quarantine — the years you spent feeling fundamentally unlike the people around you, waiting for it to either pass or consume you. Oshimi draws silence and stillness better than almost anyone, and he uses the vampire not as a power fantasy but as a metaphor for the loneliness that doesn't end when you grow up.

Skip it if you want vampire action; the pace is glacial and the violence, when it comes, is sad rather than thrilling. But if you want a horror manga that understands isolation from the inside — the way it isolates further the more it deepens — this is one of the most quietly devastating uses of the genre you'll find.

<!--SPOILER-->
What elevates Happiness above its premise is Oshimi's refusal to let transformation be liberation. Makoto doesn't become powerful; he becomes alone in a new and total way, and the people who try to reach him — Yuki, Nora — can't follow him across the gap. The long time-skips toward the end turn the vampire's curse into a study of grief that outlasts everyone, the unbearable arithmetic of immortality: you keep, and you keep losing.
