A teenage professional shogi player, orphaned young and carrying a quiet, unnamed depression, drifts through a life that's all obligation and no warmth — until a family of three sisters across the river begins, without fuss, to pull him back toward the living. Umino draws his interior life with a precision that can stop you cold; the loneliness is rendered so accurately it doubles as recognition.

Read this on a night you want to feel understood rather than entertained. The shogi matches are tense and beautifully staged, but the real game is internal: the slow, non-linear work of letting other people matter to you again. The art swings between stark emotional abstraction and the simple warmth of a shared meal, and both register fully.

If you want pace or plot, this is the wrong book; it moves like recovery does, in small steps and setbacks. But if you're carrying something heavy and want company rather than a fix, March Comes in Like a Lion is one of the most compassionate manga ever made — a story that treats healing as the genuine achievement it is.

<!--SPOILER-->
The genius of the structure is how Umino refuses a clean cure. Rei's depression doesn't lift on a schedule; it recedes and returns, and the Kawamoto sisters aren't a magic solution so much as a reason to keep showing up. The bullying arc involving Hina is the series' moral center — Rei's decision to stand by her becomes the first time he chooses to act for someone else, and that choice, not any tournament result, is what actually moves him toward being well.
