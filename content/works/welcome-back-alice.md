Oshimi has spent his career finding the horror inside ordinary adolescence, and here he goes straight for the foundation: who you are, what you want, and whether either question has a stable answer. A childhood friend returns transformed, and the comfortable triangle of three teenagers becomes a slow, vertiginous interrogation of identity, attraction, and the lies bodies tell. It is provocative in the way Oshimi is always provocative — not to shock, but to dislodge.

Read this on the night you are willing to have your assumptions about gender and desire treated not as politics but as existential weather — something that moves through a person and remakes them. It is uncomfortable on purpose. The discomfort is the experience: that creeping sense that the categories you stand on are thinner than you thought.

Skip it if you want a clean, affirming answer; Oshimi deals in destabilization, not resolution, and some readers find his gaze on teenage bodies hard to sit with. But if you can meet it on its terms, it is one of the boldest things in mainstream seinen — a manga willing to make adolescence genuinely strange again, instead of nostalgic.

<!--SPOILER-->
The reveal-by-reveal unraveling of who Alice is and what each character actually wants refuses to land on a tidy thesis. Oshimi's interest isn't in a "twist" but in keeping the ground moving — every time you think you've located the truth of these kids, the manga shifts and shows you the desire was never where you put it. The ending leaves identity as an open wound rather than a resolved question, which is exactly the point: he is drawing the age at which nothing about yourself is settled yet, and pretending otherwise is the real horror.
