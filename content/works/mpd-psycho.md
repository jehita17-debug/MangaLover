A detective with dissociative identity disorder hunts serial killers — and finds that the deeper he goes, the less stable the line is between the investigator and the things he's investigating. Otsuka's scripting is dense, allusive, and deliberately disorienting, while Tajima's art is some of the most coldly beautiful gore in the medium. Together they make a thriller that's as interested in the philosophy of a fractured self as in the mechanics of the crimes.

Read this on the night you're drawn to the unsettling idea that "you" might be a committee rather than a person — that identity is something assembled and maintained, and could come apart. The plotting can be willfully obscure, the conspiracy operatic, but the central image stays with you: a mind that contains more than one occupant, investigating a world that keeps producing them.

Skip it if extreme, explicit violence is off the table, or if you need a mystery that plays fully fair. This one earns its reputation for being difficult and graphic. But if you want crime fiction that treats the detective's own consciousness as the real crime scene — and an art style that makes horror look like a gallery piece — there's nothing else quite like it.

<!--SPOILER-->
The series uses its conspiracy scaffolding less to "solve" anything than to keep destabilizing the idea of a unified self — the barcoded eyes, the swapped personalities, the sense that agency is being authored from elsewhere all serve one theme: that identity is a story the brain tells to survive, and the story can be rewritten. The narrative's notorious opacity is partly the point; you're meant to leave it unsure which self was ever driving, which is the same uncertainty the protagonist lives inside.
