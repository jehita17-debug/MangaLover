A man with a reptile head and no memory hunts through a rotting city for the sorcerer who did this to him — and Hayashida uses that pulpy setup to build one of the most fully imagined worlds in manga, a place of smog and gyoza and casual dismemberment that somehow feels lived-in and almost cozy. The violence is constant and matter-of-fact, but the tone is strangely affectionate; these are monsters who get hungry, who have friends, who carry grudges and packed lunches.

Read this on the night you want to disappear into a world that operates on its own dream logic — where the rules are strange, the imagery is grotesque and gorgeous, and the people you'd expect to be villains turn out to be the ones you root for. The plotting is dense and the cast is huge, but the texture is so distinctive that getting lost is the pleasure.

Skip it if you need clean heroes or squeamish about gore played almost for comedy. But if you want dark fantasy that's genuinely inventive rather than just grim — that finds tenderness and absurdity inside the carnage — Dorohedoro is a singular thing, drawn by an artist who clearly built every alley of this city in her head before she let you in.

<!--SPOILER-->
The slow untangling of Caiman's identity — that the head, the amnesia, and the man inside the man are a single buried crime — pays off because Hayashida cares more about the found family than the mystery. By the end the "who did this" question matters less than the bonds formed chasing it, and the resolution insists that even in a world this cruel, loyalty and appetite and stubborn affection are what actually structure a life. It's a dark fantasy whose final note is, improbably, communal warmth.
