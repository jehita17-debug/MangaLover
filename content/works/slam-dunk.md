A red-haired delinquent joins the basketball team for the worst reason — a crush — and slowly, almost against his will, becomes someone. That's the spine, and decades on it still holds, because Inoue draws people before he draws athletes. The comedy is broad, the rivalries are real, and somewhere around the midpoint the jokes quietly give way to something that can put a lump in your throat.

Read this when you want the feeling of a thing clicking into place for someone who'd never had it. Sakuragi is all bluster, and watching the game strip that away to reveal genuine hunger is the whole pleasure. The late-game stretches are paced with a control that newer sports manga still study.

It's an older work, and you can feel the era in its rhythms and humor. If you only read for the polish of modern art, the early chapters may feel rough. Push through. Slam Dunk isn't recommended out of nostalgia — it's recommended because it remains one of the most emotionally honest sports stories ever drawn, and the ending lands harder than almost anything that came after.
