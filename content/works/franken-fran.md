Fran is a patchwork surgeon who will grant any request — heal anyone, save anyone, fix anything — and she does, flawlessly, which is precisely the horror. Each mostly self-contained chapter watches a desperate person get exactly what they asked for and discover that getting what you want is its own kind of damnation. Kigitsu draws genuinely upsetting body-horror, but the cruelty is always in the logic, not the gore: be careful what you wish for, rendered with surgical literalism.

Read this on the night you want short, sharp doses of dread you can finish in one sitting — and a protagonist whose total sincerity makes the outcomes worse. Fran isn't malicious; she is purely, innocently devoted to fulfilling requests, and that innocence is what turns each story into a small ethical trap. The ironies are clean, the comeuppances are brutal, and the worldview underneath is bleakly funny.

Skip it if graphic surgical horror is a hard limit. But if you want a horror anthology that's smarter than its shock value — that uses the monster-of-the-week format to keep asking what people actually mean when they say "save them," "make them love me," "bring them back" — this one will leave a mark, one chapter at a time.

<!--SPOILER-->
The recurring engine is dramatic irony at its most merciless: Fran fulfills the letter of every wish while the spirit of it curdles, and because she never intends cruelty, there's no villain to blame — only the gap between what people ask for and what they need. The strongest chapters quietly indict the wisher, not the surgeon, and the rare moments where Fran's own longing for connection surfaces give the anthology a melancholy throughline beneath the grand-guignol: even the one who can grant any wish can't operate her way into being loved.
